Temperature


ddsds

Information

  • Measures air temperature in Fahrenheit (°F).
  • Range: -40°F to 185°F (-40°C to 85°C).
  • A human can survive at 140°F for 10 minutes Source.

Humidity


ddsds

Information

  • Measured in Relative Humidity percentage (% RH).
  • Indicates water vapor concentration (from 0% RH to 100% RH) in the air compared to the maximum amount it can hold at a specific temperature (saturated air has 100% humidity).
  • When humidity is near 100% RH, water vapor condenses and forms fog near the surface (or clouds in the sky).

Atmospheric Pressure


ddsds

Information

  • Measures air pressure on Earth’s surface (or other surfaces) in Pascals which indicates air’s force on a square meter. It also shows altitude from sea level.
  • Range: 30,000Pa to 110,000Pa (Altitude Range: 0 to 30,000 ft).
  • 101,325 Pa is the average pressure at sea-level and fluctuates depending on weather conditions (wind velocity, gravity, temperature, etc).
  • The higher the altitude is, the lower the atmospheric pressure will be, leading to altitude sickness due to the lack of oxygen in the air.

tVOC


ddsds

Information

  • Measures the amount of Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) in the air in parts per billion (ppb).
  • Range: 0 to 32,768 parts per billion.
  • VOCs are pollutants and/or sensory irritants that come from a variety of sources like trees, animals, or constructions.
  • Although VOCs are not used to calculate Air Quality Index, they are helpful to predict photochemical smog. High levels of VOCs (tVOC over 5 ppb) can cause irritation and allergy in sensitive people, or even headaches, loss of coordination and nauseaSource.
  • Exposing to air with tVOC over 100ppb in long-term can cause nasal cancerSource.

eCO2


ddsds

Information

  • Another scale to measure Volatile Organic Compounds. Mainly used to measure the greenhouse effect of VOCs compared to CO2.
  • eCO2 sensing from 400 parts per million (ppm) to 29,206 ppm.

  • Detection of Gamma and Beta radiation recorded in one-millionth of a sievert per hour (uSv/h). Sievert quantifies the amount of ionizing radiation absorbed by human tissues.
  • Measurement range of 0.05uSv/h to 10mSv/h at 0.01 counts per minute (cpm) to 300Kcpm with a required measurement time of two minutes.
  • Examples of some radiation reading of whole dosage in millisievert (uSv) Source:
    • Typical dosage recorded in those Chernobyl workers who died within a month: 6,000,000 (uSv)
    • Single dosage which would cause radiation sickness, or cause fatal cancer many years late in 5% of people: 1,000,000 (uSv)
    • Max radiation levels recorded at Fukushima plant, per hour: 400,000 (uSv)
